## A Line Built Alongside Its Own City

On 24 August 2005, a new railway line opened between Akihabara in central Tokyo and Tsukuba in Ibaraki Prefecture. Operated by Metropolitan Intercity Railway Company (首都圏新都市鉄道株式会社), established in 1991, the Tsukuba Express covers 58.3 kilometres across 20 stations at a maximum speed of 130 km/h, reaching Tsukuba from Akihabara in as little as 45 minutes. Construction began in 1994 and took approximately eleven years, at a total project cost of around 840 billion yen.

What sets Tsukuba Express apart from other commuter railways is that the line was built alongside a coordinated urban development programme. Approximately 3,300 hectares of corridor land across Chiba and Ibaraki Prefectures were subject to a unified land-readjustment scheme, so that residential, commercial, and business districts were planned and prepared in advance of the trains themselves. Rather than cities waiting for a railway to arrive, the railway and the cities were designed to grow together.

## The Stations That Became Cities

The effects on the corridor are measurable. Nagareyama Otaka-no-Mori in Chiba Prefecture combined rail access with strong municipal child-rearing support, attracting a sustained influx of young families. Kashiwa-no-ha Campus, also in Chiba, became the site of a public-private-academic smart city initiative that drew national and international attention. On the Ibaraki side, Moriya and Kenkyugakuen stations both saw sustained residential construction after opening. Corridor population reportedly grew by around 23 percent in the two decades after opening — one of the clearest indications in Japan of what coordinated railway and urban planning can produce at corridor scale.

At Akihabara, the line connects to the JR Yamanote and Chuo-Sobu lines and the Tokyo Metro Hibiya Line. Rolling stock continues to be updated, and discussions over a possible extension toward central Tokyo are under way, placing Tsukuba Express among those still in active development well into its second decade of operation.

## Summary

Tsukuba Express opened on 24 August 2005, linking Akihabara to Tsukuba across 58.3 kilometres and 20 stations at up to 130 km/h. Built in parallel with one of Japan's most comprehensive railway-corridor land-readjustment programmes — 3,300 hectares across two prefectures — the line is as much an urban planning story as a transport one. Standing on the Akihabara platform and watching the train arrive from a corridor that was largely undeveloped before the construction crews arrived makes the scale of what was built in eleven years unusually clear.
