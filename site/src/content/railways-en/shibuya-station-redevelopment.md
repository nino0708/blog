## A Terminal Built in a Bowl

Shibuya Station opened on 1 March 1885 as a stop on the Nippon Railway's Shinagawa Line — the predecessor of today's JR Yamanote Line — with around 16 passengers on its first day. Today four operators — JR East, Tokyo Metro, Tokyu Corporation, and Keio Electric Railway — run nine lines through the station. The defining physical fact is the terrain: Shibuya sits at the lowest point of a bowl-shaped valley (suribachi, literally "mortar bowl"), with the surrounding hills rising roughly 20 metres above the station area. Because each railway arrived from a different direction and elevation, platforms are distributed across more than 30 metres of vertical space: the Tokyo Metro Ginza Line runs elevated above street level, JR's Yamanote and Saikyo lines cross on a viaduct, and the Tokyu Toyoko and Tokyo Metro Fukutoshin lines run deep underground. Three structural layers — elevated, at-grade, and subterranean — occupy the same point in the city.

The bowl terrain is as much an engineering problem as a geographical one. Rainwater drains toward the low point, and past typhoons have inundated the underground concourses. The station's east side sits directly above the underground Shibuya River, and engineering a new building over it — while maintaining the river's required cross-section — required more than three years of hydraulic modelling before a workable routing could be finalised.

## Five Track Switches and a "Once-in-a-Century" Rebuild

Tokyu, JR East, and Tokyo Metro have marketed the reconstruction as a "once in a century" project, running roughly from 2012 to 2034. The visible transformation began in April 2012 with the opening of Shibuya Hikarie. On 16 March 2013, the Tokyu Toyoko Line's above-ground Shibuya terminus was closed overnight and replaced by underground tracks connecting directly to the Tokyo Metro Fukutoshin Line, enabling through-running service to Saitama and Yokohama without a change of train.

JR East began main station construction in July 2015 and has since carried out five major track-switching operations while trains continued to run. The fifth operation, completed in November 2023, raised the Yamanote Line tracks — a process known as kōjō — to create the clearance needed for a new east–west free walkway beneath the platforms. A 4,000-cubic-metre underground flood storage tank was commissioned in August 2020, directly addressing the bowl terrain's flooding weakness. A new south gate opened provisionally on 21 July 2024; the full six-storey station building is scheduled to open in March 2027. The main functional infrastructure — including the east–west free passage — is targeted for completion by fiscal 2030, with the full project, including Hachiko Square and the surrounding plazas, due by fiscal 2034.

## Summary

Shibuya Station, opened in 1885 and today served by four operators across nine lines, has been undergoing its most far-reaching transformation since opening. The suribachi terrain, a buried river course under the east exit, and over 30 metres of vertical spread between platforms have made the work among the most technically demanding urban railway engineering in Japan. With five JR track switches now complete, a new south gate open, and the full station building due in March 2027, the project is entering its closing phase — though the complete picture will not emerge until 2034. Walking through the construction hoardings at street level while trains run overhead and underground simultaneously is its own kind of structural spectacle.
