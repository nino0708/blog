## A Brick Viaduct That Never Stopped Carrying Trains

Along the Kandagawa river, between Kanda and Ochanomizu, a run of red-brick arches carries the JR Chuo Line overhead. The viaduct was built in 1912 and, well over a century later, still supports active railway traffic. Since September 2013, the space beneath these arches — and the former platform level above — has operated as mAAch ecute Kanda-Manseibashi, a commercial facility with a total floor area of approximately 1,950 square metres, owned and operated by JR East. The building's age is not incidental to the experience; it is the experience.

The history of this site begins in 1904, when Kobu Railway (predecessor to the Chuo Line) opened a provisional station here called Manseibashi. In 1912, a new red-brick station building designed by Tatsuno Kingo — the architect also responsible for the original Tokyo Station — was completed, establishing Manseibashi as a terminal. The 1923 Great Kanto Earthquake destroyed the station building. A rebuilt station opened in 1925, but wartime conditions led to its permanent closure in November 1943. The viaduct itself outlasted everything: it carried the railway through the station's entire life and continued doing so long after.

## The 1912 Staircase and What Remains Above

MIKAN Architects led the commercial fitout of the viaduct space under a principle of contrast rather than disguise: new concrete elements are placed alongside the original brick, making the distinction between old and new visible rather than erasing it. The lower level's corridor of repeating brick arches forms the main shopping passage. On the upper level, the preserved station platform remains open, including a staircase dating to 1912 — still in use, still identified by the year it was built. From the platform deck, active Central Line trains pass close enough to register the sound and motion clearly. To stand at the height where passengers once boarded is something no amount of description quite substitutes for. The facility has received the Good Design Award.

## Summary

mAAch ecute Kanda-Manseibashi opened in September 2013 inside a viaduct first built in 1912, covering approximately 1,950 square metres of floor area. Owned by JR East and designed by MIKAN Architects, the facility preserves the platform remains and the original 1912 staircase of the former Manseibashi Station — a station that opened in 1904, found its peak in 1912, and went silent in 1943. Walking from Kanda toward Ochanomizu along the Kandagawa, the brick arches are easy to pass without noticing — until you step through one and find that eighty years of silence have been converted, carefully and without erasure, into somewhere worth stopping.
