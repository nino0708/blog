## Eight Stacked Houses at the Gate

On the corner facing the giant red lantern that marks the entrance to Nakamise-dori, the Asakusa Culture Tourist Information Center opened in April 2012. The building was designed by Kengo Kuma and Associates for Taito City, and rises eight floors above ground with one below, reaching a height of 38.9 metres. Its total floor area is approximately 2,159 square metres — compact by Tokyo standards, yet unmistakable in silhouette.

Looking up at the facade, you see eight separate volumes, each wearing a pitched gable roof as though a row of traditional townhouses had been lifted and stacked one on top of the other. Each floor carries its roof at a slightly different angle, so the stack reads as a collection of individual pieces rather than a single repeating unit. Timber louvres wrap each storey, softening the steel-frame structure beneath. The building stands at what is arguably the most prominent address in Asakusa — directly opposite Kaminarimon — and responds to the historic streetscape not by mimicking the past but by reinterpreting it in contemporary materials and scale.

## The Height Negotiation That Shaped the Design

The building's final height of 38.9 metres was reached only after extended consultation with Senso-ji temple and local shopkeepers' associations, who raised concerns about the impact on the historic streetscape. The original plan called for a building of approximately 41 metres, but this was reduced following those discussions. The negotiation effectively became part of the design process: the completed roofline is said to sit within the same horizon as Senso-ji's five-storey pagoda when viewed from Nakamise-dori. Constraints absorbed into the design rather than resisted is a recurring theme in Kuma's work, and this building is often cited as an example of that approach.

The top floor is an open observation terrace offering an elevated view over Kaminarimon, Nakamise, and the approach to Senso-ji. Looking down from this height, the depth and structure of Asakusa become clear in a way that is not apparent at street level. For a building whose explicit purpose is to orient visitors to the surrounding area, the terrace delivers that function as architecture rather than just as information.

## Summary

The Asakusa Culture Tourist Information Center was completed in 2012, with eight floors above ground and one below, standing 38.9 metres tall and covering approximately 2,159 square metres. Designed by Kengo Kuma and Associates for Taito City, its stacked townhouse volumes engage directly with the character of historic Asakusa. After visiting the tourist information counter on the ground floor, the observation terrace is worth the elevator ride before you step into the flow of Nakamise-dori.
