## A Tower Born from Redevelopment

On the northern edge of Shinjuku Gyoen, the Tomihisa-cho neighborhood of Shinjuku City once held a dense cluster of narrow lanes, aging wooden buildings, and limited road access inherited from the early twentieth century. That urban fabric was replaced in 2015 by Tomihisa Cross Comfort Tower — a 55-floor residential skyscraper completed as part of the Nishi-Tomihisa District Type 1 Urban Redevelopment Project. Four companies — Nomura Real Estate, Mitsui Fudosan Residential, Sekisui House, and Hankyu Real Estate — co-developed the building. Design was carried out by Kume Sekkei, with construction by Toda Construction and Penta-Ocean Construction.

The "Cross" in the project name refers to the two-building structure of the redeveloped district: the for-sale residential tower (Comfort Tower) and a rental residential building (Green Residence) share the same site, together branded as Tomihisa Cross. This pairing makes the project a residential anchor rather than a single high-rise insertion — a neighborhood rebuilt around a place to live.

## Disaster Prevention as the Driving Force

The Nishi-Tomihisa district had long been identified as a fire and earthquake hazard zone. Narrow roads, densely packed structures, and inadequate evacuation routes made large-scale emergencies a serious concern for the area. The Type 1 Urban Redevelopment project that produced Tomihisa Cross was designed directly to address those conditions: consolidating fragmented lots, widening access routes, and creating publicly accessible open space where there had been none.

The resulting tower stands 55 floors above ground and 2 floors below, with a recorded building height of 179.95 meters (some sources note the roof structure reaches 191 meters). The total floor area reaches approximately 139,000 m². Under the rights-conversion method standard in Japanese urban redevelopment, original landholders exchanged their claims on the old buildings for proportional rights in the new structure — preserving some continuity of neighborhood ownership even as the physical environment was comprehensively rebuilt. The developer was the Nishi-Tomihisa District Urban Redevelopment Association; the four major real estate companies joined at the sales stage, drawing buyers from well beyond the original district.

## Summary

Tomihisa Cross Comfort Tower was completed in 2015, with 55 floors above ground and a height of approximately 180 meters. It stands as one of the clearer examples of how Tokyo's urban redevelopment framework can transform a hazard-prone low-rise district into a modern high-rise complex — while keeping some of the original community in place. From the paths around Shinjuku Gyoen, the tower is visible to the northwest. Walking the streets of Tomihisa-cho, where pockets of the older neighborhood scale still remain, gives the best sense of how radically — and how quickly — Tokyo rebuilds itself.
